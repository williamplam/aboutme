How Did I Get Here?

As a young 5 year old boy growing up in a family restaurant business, I had a STEM hero, although I didn’t know it at the time. Today, I can confidently say that my STEM hero was ... my 14 year old sister. 

The world needs more people like my sister. Not all women STEM heroes are engineers, scientists, doctors, or mathematicians. 

My older sister is a STEM hero because I grew up watching her fix or attempt to fix everything in the restaurant. I’d sometimes discover her fixing a broken can opener or completely disassembling a noisy blender. 

Remember that this was back in the late 1960s and early 1970s. YouTube instructional videos and 800 toll free support numbers didn’t exist. She magically seemed to know how to do everything. She was 1st line technical support for a 40 employee business. As a 5 year old, I looked up in awe at her and followed her everywhere.

One day, I asked how she knew all this. She responded with “common sense”. I didn't know what that meant and she explained that if you try hard enough, you can figure out most things on your own.

I credit my parents for encouraging her to do this. For not following stereotypes that girls can’t do this, or girls shouldn’t do that. 

Five years later as a 10 year old and with my sister off to college, I now had to fix everything. She inspired me to have the confidence to do the job and have the common sense to get things resolved.

Today with a family of my own, I wanted to provide the same hands-on problem solving opportunities for my daughters. John Yeh of Landroids suggested I start an all-girls robotics team, which led to the formation of Exit 5 Robotics and Exit 5A Robotics. In all fairness, that then led to my son's all boys robotics team, Exit 6 Robotics. 

STEM heroes for women aren’t always obvious science teachers, talented engineers, and famous women. STEM heroes are everyday people. My sister who fixed everything. My mom and my dad who didn’t care about stereotypes of the times. They are all STEM heroes.
